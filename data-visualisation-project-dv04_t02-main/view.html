Welcome

Welcome to the Our Data Visualisation Assignment. This site provides interactive visualisations of speeding offence trends across Australia, supporting road safety research and policy.

About the Data

Data Sources

  • BITRE Enforcement Dataset: The primary data comes from the Bureau of Infrastructure and Transport Research Economics (BITRE), which compiles speeding offence records from state and territory police and road safety agencies.
  • Population Dataset: To calculate fines per 10,000 people, we used a second dataset containing population estimates by state and age group. This allows for fair comparisons across regions with different population sizes.

What Each Column Means

  • Year: The year the offence was recorded.
  • Jurisdiction: The state or territory where the offence occurred (e.g., VIC, NSW, QLD).
  • Metric: The type of offence or enforcement action (e.g., speeding, camera-detected).
  • Start Date: The month and year of the offence.
  • Fines (Count): The number of fines issued.
  • Detection Method: How the offence was detected (e.g., fixed camera, mobile camera, police issued, red light camera, average speed camera).
  • Age Group: The age bracket of the driver fined (e.g., 0â€“16, 17â€“25, 26â€“39, 40â€“64, 65+).
  • Population: The estimated number of people in the relevant group (from the second dataset).
  • Fines per 10,000: The rate of fines per 10,000 people, calculated using both datasets.

Dashboard Purpose

Our goal is to make complex enforcement data accessible and meaningful. By combining offence records with population data, we enable:

  • Fair comparisons between states and age groups, regardless of size.
  • Insights into which groups or regions are most affected by speeding offences.
  • Evidence-based decisions for road safety policy and resource allocation.

How to Use the Dashboard

  • View Data (this page): Learn about the dataset, its sources, and key attributes.
  • Chart: Explore interactive visualisations showing fines by state, age group, detection method, and over time. Use the charts to compare both raw counts and population-adjusted rates.
  • Storytelling: Read a narrative summary of the most important findings and their implications for road safety.
